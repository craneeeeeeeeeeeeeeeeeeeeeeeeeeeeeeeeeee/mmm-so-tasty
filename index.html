The mechanism

How your tongue
detects flavour

Tasting food is a remarkable feat of biological engineering involving thousands of microscopic sensors working in perfect coordination with your brain.

01
Food enters your mouth

As you chew, food breaks down and mixes with saliva. This dissolves the chemical compounds in food, making them available for your taste receptors.

02
Taste buds activate

Your tongue has around 10,000 taste buds, each containing 50–150 specialized taste receptor cells. The dissolved chemicals bind to these receptor proteins.

03
Signal sent to brain

When taste molecules bind to receptors, they trigger electrical signals carried by three cranial nerves directly to the gustatory cortex of your brain.

04
Flavour is perceived

Your brain processes and combines signals from taste, smell, texture, temperature, and even sound to construct the rich experience of "flavour."

Primary sensations

The five basic tastes

Scientists have identified five distinct taste categories, each serving a unique survival purpose that evolved over millions of years.

🍯
Sweet

Signals the presence of sugars and carbohydrates — a key energy source. Our ancestors evolved to crave sweetness to identify calorie-rich foods essential for survival.

Found in → Fruit, honey, sugar
🧂
Salty

Detects sodium and mineral salts, which are critical for nerve and muscle function, fluid balance, and blood pressure regulation in the body.

Found in → Salt, olives, cheese
🍋
Sour

Triggered by acids (low pH), sourness evolved as a warning signal — spoiled food is often acidic. Yet moderate sourness (citrus) signals vitamin C and nutrition.

Found in → Lemon, vinegar, yogurt
☕
Bitter

A protective mechanism against toxins. Plants evolved bitter compounds as a defence, and humans evolved bitterness detection as a poison alarm system.

Found in → Coffee, dark chocolate, greens
🍖
Umami

The "savoury" taste, discovered in 1908 by Japanese scientist Kikunae Ikeda. It detects glutamate — an amino acid that signals protein-rich food.

Found in → Meat, mushrooms, soy sauce
The hidden sense

Taste is mostly smell

What we call "flavour" is really a multisensory illusion. Your tongue only detects basic tastes — but it's your nose doing most of the heavy lifting.

80%
of what we perceive as flavour actually comes from our sense of smell, not our tongue

When you eat, volatile aroma molecules travel up the back of your throat to your olfactory receptors — a process called retronasal olfaction. This is why food tastes bland when you have a blocked nose.

Microscopic biology

The taste bud up close

Each taste bud is a tiny onion-shaped cluster of cells nestled in the papillae — the small bumps visible on your tongue. They contain hair-like microvilli that poke through a taste pore and make direct contact with food chemicals.



When a taste molecule binds to a receptor protein, it triggers an electrochemical cascade that fires a nerve signal at up to 70 metres per second — reaching your brain almost instantly.

Surprising facts about taste

👶
Babies have more taste buds

Infants are born with taste buds not just on their tongue, but on the roof of their mouth and cheeks too — making their sense of taste far more sensitive than adults.

🧬
Supertasters are real

About 25% of people are "supertasters" — they have significantly more taste buds than average and experience flavours with far greater intensity, especially bitterness.

✈️
Food tastes different on planes

At altitude, cabin pressure and dry air reduce your sense of smell by up to 30%, and the low humidity dries out your taste buds — making food seem blander mid-flight.

🌡️
Temperature changes taste

Cold suppresses taste receptor activity — that's why ice cream tastes sweeter as it melts and warm beer tastes more bitter than cold beer.

🎵
Sound affects flavour

Research shows that high-pitched sounds enhance sweetness while low-pitched sounds enhance bitterness. Background music in restaurants genuinely changes how your food tastes.

🐟
Cats can't taste sweetness

Cats and several other carnivores lack a functional sweet taste receptor gene. Since they evolved eating meat, sweetness detection was never necessary for their survival.
